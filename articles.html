Foreign Corporations, Controlled Groups, and the Gross Receipts Test

Executive Summary
Section 1059A has reemerged as a critical issue for tax year 2025 due to increased tariffs and the continued growth of foreign-owned U.S. businesses. Historically overlooked, Section 1059A now directly impacts taxable income by limiting inventory costs to the value declared for customs purposes, creating potential upward adjustments to income where inconsistencies exist. Many businesses implemented tariff mitigation strategies without considering the tax implications, leading to misalignment between customs values and tax reporting. This misalignment not only affects taxable income but also creates significant compliance risk, particularly with respect to Form 5472, where incorrect reporting can trigger a $25,000 penalty. As a result, tax practitioners must proactively identify and reconcile differences between customs valuation, transfer pricing, and tax accounting to ensure accurate reporting and avoid substantial penalties.

The Convergence of Tariffs, Transfer Pricing, and U.S. Tax Compliance: Why Section 1059A Now Directly Impacts Taxable Income
For many foreign-owned U.S. businesses, tariffs have historically been a peripheral consideration; tariffs either did not apply or were immaterial and were relevant only to logistics teams. Tariffs were largely not considered for U.S. income tax reporting purposes and were ignored by many CPAs and tax practitioners.

That paradigm shifted in 2025 because of two converging factors: (1) a significant increase in foreign businesses incorporating U.S. businesses and (2) the Trump Administration’s broad increase in tariffs. Because of these interconnected issues, a little-known Code section enacted in the 1980s is back in the spotlight, Section 1059A.

For tax practitioners serving small and medium sized inbound businesses, understanding Section 1059A is now essential to accurate tax compliance and risk management.

The Statutory Framework
Section 1059A provides that when a U.S. taxpayer imports property from a related party, the taxpayer’s tax basis or inventory cost cannot exceed the value declared for customs purposes. In other words, Section 1059A prevents taxpayers from using lower values for customs to reduce tariffs and then using higher values for inventory and tax purposes to increase deductions. The rule ensures consistency between customs reporting and income tax reporting.

Historical Context
When Section 1059A was enacted, tariffs were a more widely used tool by the U.S. government. However, since the 1990s, tariffs have waned as a broader tool in global trade. In addition, when Section 1059A was passed, there were far fewer foreign-owned U.S. businesses. As a result, relatively few U.S. taxpayers were impacted by Section 1059A over the last 30 years.

Many tax practitioners, especially those serving small and medium-sized businesses, never encountered Section 1059A. Fast-forward to 2025, with a large increase in overarching tariffs and a trend of increasing foreign-owned U.S. businesses, tens of thousands of taxpayers could be impacted. These developments have brought Section 1059A squarely back into relevance for tax practitioners and their clients.

Why This Matters in 2025
U.S. businesses scrambled throughout 2025 to mitigate the impact of tariffs on their bottom line. In many cases, businesses were able to change their transfer pricing or use different valuation methods to lower the value of imported goods and decrease tariffs. Many tax practitioners assisted their clients in identifying and implementing these tariff mitigation strategies without regard to filing the 2025 tax returns.

Now, as tax practitioners prepare their clients’ estimates and tax returns, they may not be immediately aware of inconsistencies between customs values and the tax basis of imported goods.

This is particularly relevant for inbound foreign owners U.S. entities, where internal coordination between accounting, logistics, and tax functions is often limited.

Impact on Taxable Income
The practical effect of Section 1059A, in this context, is that it forces a downward adjustment to inventory cost and increases taxable income. Section 1059A caps the deductible inventory cost at the lower customs-based amount.

This creates tension between:
• transfer pricing (often profit-based methods),
• customs valuation (often transaction-based or deductive methods), and
• financial reporting and inventory accounting under Sections 471 and 263A.

Form 5472 Implications and Penalty Exposure
Failure to properly apply Section 1059A has direct consequences for Form 5472 reporting. Questions 37 and 38 require accurate reporting of related-party transactions, including inventory purchases and cost of goods sold.

If Section 1059A is not addressed, taxpayers may not make the appropriate adjustments for inventory or cost of goods sold, making it unlikely these questions are answered accurately. This creates exposure to penalties for a substantially incomplete Form 5472, resulting in a $25,000 penalty per form for failure to file complete and accurate information.

For foreign-owned U.S. corporations, and tax practitioners preparing returns, this is a high-risk issue that could leave practitioners on the hook.

Practical Application
You are now aware of Section 1059A and the repercussions of ignoring it. What’s next? The computation of a Section 1059A adjustment are laid out in 1.1059A and are relatively straightforward.

However, it can be difficult to locate the proper information for the calculation. Businesses can refer to customs documentation like CPB Form 7501 (entry summary). However, often times related party imports will not be segregated from imports from unrelated parties. It can be difficult to get the necessary information to calculate the 1059A adjustment. If this is the case, this should be noted on Form 5472 in a statement, with an estimates, assumptions, and methodology.

The Section 1059A adjustment should also be closely coordinated with the company’s transfer pricing and should be viewed in the totality of the business. U.S. taxpayers must satisfy the requirements of transfer pricing rules, customs rules, and Section 1059A, simultaneously.

Emerging Enforcement and Coordination Issues
Recent developments further complicate this analysis. The IRS has reiterated that Section 1059A operates independently of Section 482, meaning taxpayers must satisfy both regimes simultaneously.

In parallel, U.S. Customs and Border Protection has increased scrutiny of related-party import pricing, particularly where transfer pricing studies are used to support customs valuation. Additionally, the U.S. Court of International Trade continues to emphasize that customs valuation must reflect the economic reality, which may diverge from transfer pricing outcomes.

These developments directly impact the Section 1059A calculation. Where transfer pricing adjustments increase the intercompany mark-up, taxpayers must evaluate whether those increases exceed the customs value ceiling. Conversely, post-importation adjustments, related services, or true ups may require downward adjustments to inventory.

As a practical matter, inbound businesses and tax practitioners serving those businesses, should implement a reconciliation process that aligns customs declarations, transfer pricing policies, and tax inventory calculations. Without this coordination, taxpayers risk distorted taxable income, inaccurate reporting, and significant penalties.
